People kept asking me for advice. I kept saying I wasn't qualified.

Then I realised I'd been helping people feel seen for years — I just hadn't called it dentistry.

The reluctant helper

During my NHS training, patients started seeking me out. Not because I was the most technically skilled — though I was thorough — but because I listened differently. Colleagues would joke that I spent too much time talking with patients. "You're not a therapist, Karm," they'd say.

But something was happening in those conversations. People were telling me things they'd never told a dentist before. About how their smile affected their confidence. How they avoided photos at family gatherings. How they covered their mouth when they laughed.

I kept telling them I was just doing my job. I wasn't qualified to help with those deeper concerns. I was a dentist, not a counsellor.

The recognition

The turning point came with Mrs. Henderson. She'd been my patient for two years, coming in for routine cleanings. Always polite, always quiet. One day, she mentioned she was getting married again at 58 — her first wedding photos in decades.

"I want to feel like myself in the pictures," she said. "Not the version of me that's been hiding."

We spent the next hour talking about what that meant. When she left with a treatment plan for subtle veneers, she hugged me. "Thank you for seeing me," she said.

That's when I realised what I'd been doing all along. I wasn't just fixing teeth. I was helping people reclaim parts of themselves they'd lost.

The acceptance

I tried to ignore it at first. Stick to the clinical side. But patient after patient kept having the same experience. They'd come in for a cleaning and leave feeling understood. They'd book a simple procedure and discover it was about so much more than aesthetics.

I started to see that my "flaw" — caring too much about how people felt — wasn't a weakness. It was exactly what people needed from their dentist.

The mouth is intimate. It's where we eat, speak, kiss, smile, express joy. Working in someone's mouth requires trust. And trust, I learned, comes from being truly seen.

West Dental is born

I left the NHS system to create something different. Not because I wanted to charge more, but because I wanted to spend more time. Time to listen. Time to understand. Time to help people discover what's possible when their smile matches who they've become.

West Dental isn't about perfect teeth. It's about that moment when you look in the mirror and recognise yourself. When you smile without thinking about it. When your outer expression aligns with your inner growth.

That's what I've been helping people with all along. I just finally stopped apologising for it.

What I believe

Clarity before treatment

You should never feel rushed into a decision about your smile. We take time to understand your goals, explain your options, and let the right choice become obvious.

Function and beauty together

A beautiful smile that doesn't work properly isn't beautiful at all. Every treatment we recommend serves both form and function — how you look and how you feel.

Your story matters

Every smile has a story. Understanding yours — your concerns, your dreams, your experiences — is essential to creating treatment that truly serves you.

Trust over transactions

This isn't about selling procedures. It's about building relationships with people who trust me to help them feel more like themselves.
